Overview

  • We know as much about how learning works as we do about public health
  • But most people in higher education don't know this knowledge exists
  • A few key ideas can make lessons significantly more effective

Having crammed a dozen books into a two-day course, we will now cram that course into one hour

Cognitive Transitions

  • Benner and others studied how nurses become more proficient
    • Work replicated in many other domains
  • A (fairly) predictable series of cognitive transitions
    • People don't just know more
    • They think differently
  • We will simplify to novice, competent, and expert

Characteristics of Novices

  • Ask questions that don't make sense
  • Do things by rote
    • No real understanding cause and effect
  • Use analogies with other domains
    • Which are often misleading or inconsistent
  • Use incorrect language or use language incorrectly
  • Often over-confident
    • Dunning-Kruger Effect

Root Cause

  • Novices don't yet have a mental model of the domain

Sorry, New Zealand

Mental Model Examples

  • "Atoms are colored balls connected by springs"
  • "Atoms are little solar systems with electrons orbiting a nucleus"
  • "A computer has a CPU, memory, and a hard drive"

Mental Models

  • Not just an inextensible collection of facts
    • Mental models support explanatory extension
  • Not necessarily a subset of "the truth"
    • But eventually explainable in its terms
  • Not necessarily identical
    • "Everyone has their own London"

How to Help Novices

  • Your goal when teaching novices is not to give them lots of information
    • Because they don't yet have somewhere to put it
  • Your goal is to help them build a usable mental model

Example

  • DataCamp's introduction to the Unix shell only teaches 15 commands in 3 hours
  • But it also teaches paths, redirection, command-line arguments, history, pipes, filename patterns, and loops
  • Without a mental model that includes those concepts, the commands don't make sense

You're Done When

  • Learners ask sensible questions
  • That use jargon, and use it correctly
    • Know what terms to search for
    • Can recognize a useful answer when they see it
  • They can extrapolate
    • "There must be an option for that…"
  • They can reason backward
    • "Y has gone wrong, so I probably need to fix X"

How to Help Novices

  • The key to helping novices build mental models is to diagnose and correct misconceptions
What is 37 + 15?
a) 52 Correct
b) 42 Threw away the '1'
c) 412 Treated each column independently
d) 43 Carried into the same column
  • Each misconception needs a different correction

Assessment

  • Formative assessment used during the lesson to shape learning as it takes happens
    • Does the learner understand?
    • If not, why not?
  • Also lets learners practice skills they will need in the summative assessment (final exam)
  • Good formative assessments are:
    • Quick (so that they don't derail lesson flow)
    • Unambiguous (so that they scale)
    • Constructive (i.e., exercise more than just recall)

Models of Models

  • You have to understand your learners' mental models in order to create diagnostic formative assessments
  • If you do, you will automatically create lessons that forestall and correct misconceptions
  • You should be able to explain what misconception(s) each expected wrong answer is checking for
